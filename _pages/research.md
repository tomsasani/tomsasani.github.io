### Evolution of the mutation rate

All phenotypic diversity on Earth can be attributed to the combined actions of mutation, selection, drift and gene flow. 
Germline mutation is arguably the most important of these forces, as it provides the raw material for all other mechanisms of evolution. 
Although new mutation can prove beneficial, it is more often disruptive, and *de novo* mutations that disrupt protein-coding sequences or regulatory elements can lead to devestating genetic disease. 
Despite the fundamental importance of mutation to both evolution and health, the mechanisms that generate new mutations and the forces that influence the mutation rate remain mysterious. 
Life history and reproductive traits, such as generation time, have been shown to influence the mutation rate in humans and other mammalian species.
And many molecular processes, such as DNA replication, DNA damage repair, and recombination, can affect the rate at which new mutations accumulate.
In my own work, I've confirmed that human germline mutations occur more frequently in the male germline, and that the number of male germline mutations increases substantially with paternal age.
I've also demonstrated that this "paternal age effect" varies considerably among large human families, suggesting that genetic modifiers of the mutation rate may be segregating in human populations today.
New mutations do not necessarily need to occur in the germline of a parent to be present in most of an organism's cells, however.
Using three-generation human pedigrees, I've discovered that mutations that occur within the first few cell divisions of embryogenesis can comprise up to 10% of human *de novo* mutations.
Although genetic modifiers of the germline mutation rate almost certainly exist in humans and other species, it remains to be seen whether they can be identified and characterized using whole-genome sequencing data.

### The role of structural variation in genome evolution

The genomes of any two humans differ at a few million individual nucleotides, and most research to date has focused on the contribution of these single base-pair changes to human evolution and heatlh.
However, structural variation, which includes large deletions, duplications, inversions, and other genomic alterations, can alter a far greater amount of the genome.
In the average human genome, large structural changes affect twice as many base pairs of DNA as SNVs, but studies of the origins and effects of these changes have lagged behind those of single-nucleotide variants.
Much of the reason for this lies in the difficulty of detecting SVs using short-read sequencing methods.
